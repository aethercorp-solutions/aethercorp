Why companies still use mysql

Companies still use MySQL for a mix of practical, economic, and technical reasons—even though newer databases exist. Here’s a clear breakdown of why it remains popular:

1. It’s mature, stable, and proven

  • MySQL has been around since 1995
  • Battle-tested at massive scale (Facebook, Booking.com, Uber historically)
  • Known behavior, predictable performance, fewer surprises than newer systems

For many companies, “boring but reliable” is a feature, not a flaw.

2. Huge ecosystem & talent pool

  • Millions of developers already know MySQL
  • Tons of tools: admin GUIs, ORMs, monitoring, backups
  • Easy to hire for compared to niche or newer databases

Switching databases often costs more in people and training than in technology.

3. Excellent performance for common workloads

MySQL (especially InnoDB) is very fast for:

  • Read-heavy applications
  • Simple to moderately complex transactional workloads
  • Web apps (CRUD, APIs, dashboards)

For many businesses, MySQL is “fast enough”, which is often the optimal choice.

4. Open source & cost-effective

  • Free to use (with optional paid Oracle support)
  • Works well on inexpensive infrastructure
  • Strong managed offerings (AWS RDS, Aurora, GCP Cloud SQL, Azure)

Lower licensing risk compared to some enterprise databases.

5. Operational simplicity

  • Easy to set up, monitor, and back up
  • Well-understood failure modes
  • Good tooling for replication, clustering, and scaling reads

Teams value operational predictability over cutting-edge features.

6. Backward compatibility & legacy systems

  • Many systems were built on MySQL years ago
  • Rewriting or migrating databases is risky and expensive
  • If it works and meets requirements, there’s little incentive to change

“Don’t rewrite a working database unless you must.”

7. Strong support in frameworks and platforms

  • First-class support in frameworks like Django, Laravel, Rails, Spring
  • Default choice for many CMSs (WordPress, Magento, Drupal)
  • Easy integration with analytics, BI tools, and ETL pipelines

8. Alternatives aren’t always better

  • PostgreSQL: more features, but sometimes higher operational complexity
  • NoSQL: great for specific use cases, but poor for relational consistency
  • NewSQL: powerful, but higher cost and operational overhead

MySQL hits a sweet spot for a wide range of applications.

When companies don’t use MySQL

They usually switch when they need:

  • Complex queries & advanced SQL features → PostgreSQL
  • Massive horizontal writes → Cassandra / DynamoDB
  • Strong global consistency at scale → Spanner / CockroachDB
  • Analytical workloads → ClickHouse / BigQuery

Companies still use MySQL because it is:

stable, fast enough, cheap, widely known, easy to operate, and low-risk.
