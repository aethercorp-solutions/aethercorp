Radar (Radio Detection And Ranging)

Radar (Radio Detection And Ranging) works by sending out radio waves and listening for their echoes when those waves bounce off objects.

Here‚Äôs a clear, step-by-step explanation:

1. Transmit radio waves üì°

A radar system sends a short burst (pulse) of radio waves using a transmitter and antenna.

  • Radio waves travel at the speed of light (~300,000 km/s).
  • The pulse spreads out in space.

2. Waves hit an object

When the radio waves encounter an object (airplane, ship, car, rain, terrain):

  • Some of the energy reflects back toward the radar.
  • This reflected signal is called an echo.

The amount reflected depends on:

  • Object size
  • Shape
  • Material (metal reflects well)
  • Orientation

3. Receive the echo

The radar antenna switches to receive mode and captures the returning echo.

The system measures:

  • Time delay between transmission and reception
  • Strength of the returned signal
  • Frequency shift (if the object is moving)

4. Calculate distance (range)

Distance is computed using the time delay:


Distance = Speed of light x Time delay

Why divide by 2?

Because the signal travels to the object and back.

5. Determine direction (bearing)

The antenna:

  • Rotates (or electronically steers the beam)
  • Knows exactly which direction it was pointing when the echo returned

That gives the angle of the object.

6. Measure speed (Doppler effect) üöó‚úàÔ∏è

If the object is moving:

  • The frequency of the returned signal shifts slightly
  • This is the Doppler effect
  • Higher frequency ‚Üí object approaching
  • Lower frequency ‚Üí object moving away

Used heavily in:

  • Speed guns
  • Weather radar
  • Aircraft tracking

7. Display the result

The processed data is shown as:

  • Dots or blips on a screen
  • Maps (weather radar)
  • Tracked targets (air traffic control, military radar)
