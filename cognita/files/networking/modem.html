Modem

A modem (short for modulator-demodulator) is a device that connects your home or office network to your Internet Service Provider (ISP), allowing you to access the internet. It translates digital data from your computer into analog signals that can travel over phone lines, cable systems, or fiber optics—and vice versa.

Types of Modems:

  1. Cable Modem – Used for broadband internet via cable TV lines.
  2. DSL Modem – Connects via telephone lines.
  3. Fiber Modem (ONT) – Optical Network Terminal for fiber optic connections.
  4. Dial-Up Modem – Outdated; uses phone lines for slow internet.
  5. Cellular Modem (or MiFi) – Connects via mobile data networks (4G/5G).

Key Functions:

  • Modulation: Converts digital signals into analog for transmission.
  • Demodulation: Converts received analog signals back into digital.
  • Authentication: Verifies your account with the ISP.
  • IP Address Assignment: Communicates with the ISP to assign an IP address to your network.

Modem vs Router:

  • A modem connects you to the internet.
  • A router connects your devices to each other and to the modem.
  • Some devices are modem-router combos, combining both functions.
